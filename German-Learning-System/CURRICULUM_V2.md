# Curriculum V2

## Product Blueprint

German Learning System is a communication-first curriculum for adult learners who want to use German in real life. The curriculum is organized around situations, topics, and learner outcomes. Grammar is introduced as a tool for communication, not as the primary organizing structure.

The product promise is that learners will move from disconnected rules and vocabulary into grounded, usable German. Each level helps learners participate in increasingly complex situations with more independence, confidence, and nuance.

## Curriculum Design Principles

1. Communication comes first.
   Every unit begins with something the learner needs to do in German: ask for help, explain a problem, make a plan, tell a story, give an opinion, resolve a misunderstanding, or participate in a conversation.

2. Grammar serves real use.
   Grammar topics are sequenced around what learners need in order to communicate. A learner meets a structure because it helps them say something meaningful.

3. Topics repeat with depth.
   Core life topics appear across levels. At A1, the learner may ask for bread at a bakery. At B2, the learner may discuss food preferences, health choices, sustainability, or cultural habits.

4. Confidence is built through action.
   Each level includes speaking and writing outcomes that can be observed. Learners should know what they can do now that they could not do before.

5. Adult learners need practical relevance.
   Lessons should feel useful for daily life, work, family, study, travel, healthcare, community, and social connection.

## Level A1: Survival Communication

### Level Role

A1 helps learners enter German-speaking environments with basic confidence. The focus is on simple interactions, clear personal information, everyday needs, and short prepared conversations.

### Main Learning Goals

- Introduce oneself and exchange basic personal information.
- Understand and use common phrases in predictable situations.
- Ask simple questions and give short answers.
- Navigate basic shopping, food, travel, and appointment situations.
- Build the first layer of sentence structure, pronunciation awareness, and everyday vocabulary.
- Develop the habit of speaking before everything feels perfect.

### Real-Life Situations

- Introducing yourself to a neighbor, classmate, colleague, or host.
- Ordering coffee, food, or baked goods.
- Shopping for groceries, clothing, or household basics.
- Asking for prices, opening hours, locations, and simple help.
- Making or confirming a simple appointment.
- Visiting the Apotheke for a basic need.
- Talking about family, home, daily routines, and free time.
- Using public transport and asking where something is.
- Filling out basic forms with name, address, phone number, and nationality.

### Core Grammar Topics

- Present tense of common regular and irregular verbs.
- Verb position in statements and yes/no questions.
- W-questions: wer, was, wo, woher, wohin, wann, wie, warum.
- Personal pronouns and basic subject-verb agreement.
- Definite and indefinite articles in nominative and accusative.
- Basic noun gender and plural awareness.
- Negation with nicht and kein.
- Modal verbs for simple needs: moechte, kann, muss.
- Separable verbs in daily routines.
- Basic prepositions for place and time.
- Numbers, dates, times, and prices.

### Speaking Outcomes

By the end of A1, learners can:

- Introduce themselves and ask another person basic questions.
- Order food or drinks using polite phrases.
- Ask for an item in a shop and understand a simple price.
- Say what they need at an Apotheke using short prepared phrases.
- Describe a basic daily routine.
- Say where they live, where they are from, and what they do.
- Ask for repetition, clarification, or slower speech.
- Participate in a short, predictable conversation with support.

### Writing Outcomes

By the end of A1, learners can:

- Complete simple personal-information forms.
- Write a short self-introduction.
- Write a simple text message about plans, time, or location.
- Write a basic shopping list or appointment note.
- Write short sentences about family, home, daily life, and preferences.

### Vocabulary Themes

- Personal information and introductions.
- Numbers, time, dates, and prices.
- Greetings and polite phrases.
- Food, drinks, cafes, bakeries, and groceries.
- Shopping basics.
- Family and relationships.
- Home and rooms.
- Daily routines.
- Transportation and directions.
- Basic health and Apotheke language.

## Level A2: Everyday Participation

### Level Role

A2 helps learners handle everyday life with more independence. The focus is on short conversations, simple explanations, past experiences, preferences, plans, and common problems.

### Main Learning Goals

- Communicate in routine daily situations with less preparation.
- Talk about past events, future plans, preferences, and needs.
- Handle practical tasks such as appointments, returns, housing questions, and simple workplace interactions.
- Understand short everyday messages, signs, announcements, and simple conversations.
- Build confidence across repeated topic areas.

### Real-Life Situations

- Explaining symptoms and asking for medicine at the Apotheke or doctor's office.
- Returning or exchanging an item in a shop.
- Making, changing, or canceling an appointment.
- Talking with a landlord, neighbor, or service provider about a household issue.
- Describing a recent trip, weekend, meal, or family event.
- Planning a meetup with a friend or colleague.
- Asking for help when something is lost, broken, late, or unclear.
- Reading short emails, notices, school messages, and service updates.
- Participating in small talk about weather, work, family, and plans.

### Core Grammar Topics

- Perfect tense for common past events.
- Prateritum of sein, haben, and modal verbs.
- Modal verbs for obligation, permission, ability, and preference.
- Dative case for recipients, locations, and common phrases.
- Two-way prepositions for location and direction.
- Possessive articles in common contexts.
- Comparative and superlative forms for everyday choices.
- Word order with time, manner, and place.
- Subordinate clauses with weil, dass, wenn.
- Imperative forms for instructions and advice.
- Reflexive verbs in daily routines and health.

### Speaking Outcomes

By the end of A2, learners can:

- Explain a simple problem and ask for a solution.
- Describe symptoms and understand basic advice.
- Talk about what happened yesterday, last week, or on a trip.
- Make plans and negotiate time, place, and preferences.
- Ask follow-up questions in familiar conversations.
- Handle a return, exchange, or simple complaint.
- Describe their home, work, family, routines, and needs.
- Keep a short conversation going with prepared strategies.

### Writing Outcomes

By the end of A2, learners can:

- Write short emails to make or change appointments.
- Write a simple message explaining a delay, problem, or request.
- Describe a past event in a short paragraph.
- Write basic notes to neighbors, teachers, coworkers, or service providers.
- Complete forms that ask for practical details.
- Write simple opinions and preferences with reasons.

### Vocabulary Themes

- Health, symptoms, medicine, and appointments.
- Shopping, returns, sizes, colors, and prices.
- Housing, repairs, furniture, and neighborhood.
- Work routines, schedules, and simple workplace needs.
- Travel, hotels, transport, and directions.
- Weather, seasons, and plans.
- Family events and social invitations.
- Food, cooking, restaurants, and preferences.
- Basic education and course language.

## Level B1: Independent Daily Life

### Level Role

B1 helps learners become independent communicators. The focus is on handling common real-life situations, explaining opinions and experiences, telling connected stories, solving problems, and participating in community, work, and social life.

### Main Learning Goals

- Communicate independently in familiar but varied situations.
- Express opinions, reasons, hopes, concerns, and preferences.
- Tell stories with clear sequence and useful detail.
- Handle misunderstandings, practical problems, and service interactions.
- Understand the main points of clear speech and everyday written texts.
- Build a stable foundation for work, community, and longer conversations.

### Real-Life Situations

- Having a conversation at a cafe with someone newly met.
- Explaining a problem to a doctor, landlord, school office, or employer.
- Discussing work tasks, availability, expectations, and feedback.
- Giving advice to a friend or asking for advice.
- Describing experiences with travel, housing, learning, work, or family.
- Participating in parent-school, course, workplace, or community conversations.
- Understanding official letters or service messages with support.
- Comparing options and making decisions.
- Expressing disagreement politely and asking for clarification.

### Core Grammar Topics

- Review and expansion of perfect and Prateritum for storytelling.
- Future meaning with werden and present-tense context.
- Subordinate clauses with weil, dass, wenn, obwohl, damit.
- Relative clauses for describing people, places, and things.
- Infinitive clauses with zu.
- Passive voice for processes and official language.
- Konjunktiv II for polite requests, wishes, advice, and hypothetical situations.
- Genitive awareness in written and formal contexts.
- Adjective endings in common nominative, accusative, and dative patterns.
- Connectors for sequence, contrast, cause, and result.
- Indirect questions for polite communication.

### Speaking Outcomes

By the end of B1, learners can:

- Sustain a conversation on familiar topics without scripting every sentence.
- Tell a connected story about an experience or problem.
- Explain what they need and why in practical situations.
- Give opinions with reasons and respond to another person's view.
- Ask for clarification when communication breaks down.
- Make polite requests and negotiate simple outcomes.
- Participate in workplace, school, healthcare, and housing conversations.
- Speak with growing confidence in spontaneous everyday encounters.

### Writing Outcomes

By the end of B1, learners can:

- Write structured emails for requests, explanations, complaints, and appointments.
- Write a short personal or professional message with context and purpose.
- Describe experiences, plans, problems, and opinions in connected paragraphs.
- Summarize the main point of a conversation, article, or notice.
- Write simple application-related texts such as a short profile or cover note.
- Use connectors to make writing clearer and more coherent.

### Vocabulary Themes

- Work, jobs, applications, schedules, and workplace communication.
- Healthcare, appointments, insurance, symptoms, and advice.
- Housing, contracts, repairs, neighbors, and moving.
- Education, courses, schools, feedback, and learning goals.
- Social life, invitations, friendships, and community.
- Travel problems, customer service, and planning.
- Feelings, opinions, preferences, and personal values.
- Media, news basics, and everyday public information.

## Level B2: Confident Participation

### Level Role

B2 helps learners participate with confidence in complex daily, professional, and social situations. The focus is on nuance, argumentation, detailed explanation, problem-solving, and adapting language to audience and context.

### Main Learning Goals

- Communicate fluently enough for active participation in work, study, and community life.
- Explain complex situations with detail, sequence, cause, and consequence.
- Express and defend opinions while responding to counterarguments.
- Understand longer spoken and written texts on familiar and semi-specialized topics.
- Adapt tone for formal, informal, professional, and service contexts.
- Reduce dependence on translation and last-minute preparation.

### Real-Life Situations

- Participating in a workplace meeting or course discussion.
- Explaining a complex problem to a service provider, institution, or employer.
- Discussing health decisions, treatment options, or insurance issues.
- Comparing housing, work, education, or financial options.
- Handling disagreement, feedback, and negotiation.
- Understanding articles, reports, policies, contracts, and official communication.
- Presenting a project, personal experience, or viewpoint.
- Participating in social conversations with humor, nuance, and follow-up questions.
- Advocating for oneself or someone else in a practical situation.

### Core Grammar Topics

- Advanced word order for emphasis and clarity.
- Expanded connector system for argumentation and structure.
- Konjunktiv II for diplomacy, speculation, and unreal conditions.
- Konjunktiv I awareness for reported speech in media and formal writing.
- Passive voice in different tenses and alternatives to passive.
- Nominalization in formal and professional German.
- Participle constructions and reduced clauses in written German.
- Advanced adjective endings and noun-phrase precision.
- Prepositional verbs and fixed expressions.
- Modal particles for tone and conversational nuance.
- Sentence linking across longer spoken and written turns.

### Speaking Outcomes

By the end of B2, learners can:

- Participate actively in discussions on familiar and practical topics.
- Explain a complex situation clearly, including background and consequences.
- Present an opinion and respond to agreement, disagreement, or questions.
- Negotiate politely in work, service, housing, or institutional contexts.
- Give a structured short presentation.
- Ask precise follow-up questions and clarify assumptions.
- Adapt speaking style for friends, colleagues, officials, and service providers.
- Recover from misunderstandings without losing the conversation.

### Writing Outcomes

By the end of B2, learners can:

- Write clear formal and semi-formal emails.
- Write a structured complaint, request, proposal, or explanation.
- Summarize and respond to articles, reports, or institutional information.
- Write opinion texts with reasons, examples, and counterpoints.
- Prepare professional or academic communication with appropriate tone.
- Use paragraphs, connectors, and precise vocabulary to guide the reader.

### Vocabulary Themes

- Professional communication, meetings, projects, and feedback.
- Institutions, bureaucracy, contracts, rights, and responsibilities.
- Healthcare systems, treatment, insurance, and wellbeing.
- Education, training, qualifications, and career development.
- Housing markets, finances, services, and consumer decisions.
- News, society, culture, and public debate.
- Technology, media, and digital communication.
- Relationships, conflict, negotiation, and social nuance.

## Level C1: Flexible, Nuanced Communication

### Level Role

C1 helps learners communicate with flexibility, precision, and independence across personal, professional, academic, and cultural contexts. The focus is on nuance, register, persuasive communication, complex texts, and confident participation in demanding situations.

### Main Learning Goals

- Communicate fluently and spontaneously across a wide range of topics.
- Understand implicit meaning, tone, register, and cultural context.
- Produce clear, well-structured spoken and written German for complex purposes.
- Adapt language to professional, academic, social, and institutional settings.
- Express subtle opinions, emotions, uncertainty, critique, and persuasion.
- Use German as a tool for full participation, not only survival or study.

### Real-Life Situations

- Leading or contributing meaningfully in professional meetings.
- Giving presentations, arguments, and recommendations.
- Reading and responding to complex official, academic, or professional texts.
- Writing persuasive emails, reports, proposals, and reflective pieces.
- Participating in nuanced conversations about culture, identity, society, work, and relationships.
- Handling conflict, sensitive topics, and ambiguity with diplomacy.
- Understanding humor, implication, idioms, and register shifts.
- Advocating for a position in institutional or professional contexts.
- Building relationships through natural, flexible conversation.

### Core Grammar Topics

- Register-sensitive grammar choices in formal and informal German.
- Advanced sentence architecture for precision and flow.
- Fine distinctions among connectors and discourse markers.
- Expanded reported speech and stance marking.
- Advanced passive alternatives and impersonal constructions.
- Nominal style in academic, official, and professional writing.
- Idiomatic prepositional patterns and collocations.
- Subtle uses of modal particles and tone softeners.
- Complex relative, participle, and infinitive constructions.
- Advanced tense and mood choices for nuance, distance, and emphasis.
- Editing for clarity, concision, and naturalness.

### Speaking Outcomes

By the end of C1, learners can:

- Speak fluently on complex topics with clear structure and natural transitions.
- Adjust tone for professional, social, academic, and formal settings.
- Express nuanced opinions and respond thoughtfully to opposing views.
- Lead discussions, ask precise questions, and synthesize other people's points.
- Handle sensitive or high-stakes conversations with diplomacy.
- Explain abstract ideas using examples, comparisons, and qualifications.
- Participate naturally in extended conversations without relying on memorized scripts.
- Use German to connect, influence, collaborate, and belong.

### Writing Outcomes

By the end of C1, learners can:

- Write polished formal, professional, and reflective texts.
- Produce structured arguments with nuance, evidence, and counterargument.
- Write reports, proposals, summaries, and recommendations.
- Adapt tone for institutions, employers, colleagues, friends, and public audiences.
- Revise writing for precision, flow, register, and impact.
- Use complex grammar and vocabulary without sacrificing clarity.
- Communicate personal voice in German with confidence and control.

### Vocabulary Themes

- Professional strategy, leadership, collaboration, and decision-making.
- Academic and analytical language.
- Society, culture, politics, identity, and public discourse.
- Conflict resolution, diplomacy, persuasion, and negotiation.
- Advanced healthcare, legal, financial, and institutional language.
- Media literacy, technology, and ethics.
- Emotional nuance, relationships, humor, and idiomatic expression.
- Style, register, tone, and rhetorical language.

The same topic areas recur across levels so learners build depth instead of collecting disconnected units.

| Health | Ask for basic medicine | Explain symptoms | Discuss treatment and appointments | Compare options and insurance issues | Navigate complex medical and institutional communication |
| Shopping | Ask for items and prices | Return or exchange items | Resolve service problems | Discuss consumer choices and rights | Argue, negotiate, and write formal complaints |
| Housing | Describe home | Report a simple repair | Handle landlord or neighbor issues | Compare contracts and services | Manage complex housing, legal, or financial communication |
| Work | Say job and schedule | Explain tasks and availability | Participate in workplace conversations | Discuss projects, feedback, and negotiation | Lead, persuade, summarize, and strategize |
| Food | Order and shop | Discuss preferences and recipes | Talk about habits and invitations | Discuss culture, health, and sustainability | Use food as a cultural and social discussion topic |
| Daily Life | Describe routines | Explain plans and problems | Tell stories and make decisions | Handle complex logistics | Navigate nuanced social and institutional life |

## Recommended Unit Architecture

Each unit should include:

- Situation: The real-life context the learner is preparing for.
- Can-do outcome: The observable communication result.
- Language toolkit: Vocabulary, phrases, grammar, and pronunciation needed for the situation.
- Input: A short dialogue, message, notice, article, audio script, or real-world text.
- Guided practice: Controlled activities that help learners notice and reuse patterns.
- Speaking task: A role play, conversation, explanation, or discussion.
- Writing task: A message, form, email, note, summary, opinion, or formal text.
- Review loop: Retrieval practice that brings back older language in the new situation.
- Transfer task: A realistic action the learner could do outside the lesson.

## Product Success Indicators

The curriculum is working when learners can say:

- I know what this lesson helps me do.
- I can connect new German to German I already know.
- I can speak before I know every word.
- I can handle real situations with less preparation.
- I can see my progress across topics and levels.
- German feels more coherent, practical, and possible.
