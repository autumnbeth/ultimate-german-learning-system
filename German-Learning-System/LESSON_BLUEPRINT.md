# Lesson Blueprint

## 1. Purpose

The Lesson Blueprint is the reusable framework that turns the German Learning System architecture into lessons.

It connects the system's core documents:

- `VISION.md`: the promise of turning scattered knowledge into usable communication.
- `LEARNING_MODEL.md`: the learner journey from real-life situation to confidence.
- `GRAMMAR_MAP.md`: the connected grammar system.
- `SENTENCE_ARCHITECTURE_MAP.md`: how German sentences are built and how ideas connect.
- `COMMUNICATION_FUNCTIONS.md`: what learners need to do with German.

Every lesson should connect:

- Real-life situations
- Communication functions
- Vocabulary
- Phrase patterns
- Grammar
- Sentence architecture
- Speaking
- Writing
- Retrieval practice
- Confidence building

The system philosophy is:

- Start with communication, not grammar.
- Start with a real-life situation, not vocabulary lists.
- Grammar exists to support communication.
- Sentence architecture exists to connect ideas.
- Confidence is a primary learning outcome.
- Every lesson should help learners do something meaningful in German.

### Real-Life Situation

Purpose: Anchor the lesson in a recognizable adult-life context.

Why it matters: Learners need to know where the language belongs before they are asked to study it.

Design principles:

- Make the situation specific.
- Choose contexts learners are likely to encounter.
- Use the situation to create a natural need for grammar, vocabulary, and sentence structure.
- Avoid beginning with a rule or list.

Examples:

- You are at the Apotheke and need help choosing something for a cough.
- You need to email a teacher because your child is absent.
- You are in a work meeting and need to ask a clarifying question.
- You missed an appointment and need to explain why.

### Communication Goal

Purpose: Define what the learner can do by the end of the lesson.

Why it matters: The goal keeps the lesson focused on capability instead of content coverage.

Design principles:

- Use a can-do statement.
- Make the outcome observable.
- Connect the goal to speaking, writing, or both.
- Keep the goal appropriate for the CEFR level.

Examples:

- I can ask for help at an Apotheke and describe one symptom.
- I can change an appointment and give a reason.
- I can write a short email explaining a problem.
- I can give my opinion and support it with an example.

### Communication Functions

Purpose: Identify the actions the learner performs with the language.

Why it matters: Functions are the bridge between language knowledge and real-life use.

Design principles:

- Choose one primary function.
- Add one to three supporting functions.
- Reuse functions across different topics and levels.
- Connect each function to phrases, grammar, sentence architecture, and confidence.

Examples:

- asking for help
- explaining a problem
- giving reasons
- making suggestions
- agreeing and disagreeing
- telling a past event

### Vocabulary Layer

Purpose: Give the learner the words needed to act in the situation.

Why it matters: Vocabulary should support use, not become an isolated memorization task.

Design principles:

- Keep the word count limited.
- Group vocabulary by use.
- Include articles and plurals when helpful.
- Put words inside phrases or example sentences.
- Separate required vocabulary from optional expansion.

Examples:

- Apotheke: die Halsschmerzen, der Husten, das Fieber, etwas gegen...
- Appointment: der Termin, verschieben, absagen, frei, passen
- Housing: die Heizung, kaputt, reparieren, der Vermieter, dringend

### Phrase Pattern Layer

Purpose: Give learners reusable chunks they can use before they can build every sentence independently.

Why it matters: Phrase patterns reduce friction and make early communication possible.

Design principles:

- Include phrases for starting, continuing, repairing, and ending the interaction.
- Use adaptable slots.
- Include polite forms where appropriate.
- Connect phrases to communication functions.

Examples:

- Können Sie mir bitte helfen?
- Ich habe ein Problem mit...
- Ich möchte einen Termin...
- Ich kann leider nicht kommen, weil...
- Wäre es möglich, ...?

### Speaking Layer

Purpose: Move the learner from recognition into spoken use.

Why it matters: Confidence grows through supported attempts, not silent understanding.

Design principles:

- Start with repetition and substitution.
- Move toward guided role play.
- Include repair phrases.
- Make the speaking task match the communication goal.

Examples:

- Repeat useful phrases with different symptoms.
- Ask and answer appointment questions.
- Role-play a short conversation with a landlord.
- Explain a problem in three sentences.

### Writing Layer

Purpose: Help the learner organize language into a useful written form.

Why it matters: Adult learners often need written German for emails, forms, messages, exams, school, work, and offices.

Design principles:

- Use realistic formats.
- Provide a model first.
- Give sentence starters.
- Include a revision checklist.
- Match tone to context.

Examples:

- Write a short message changing an appointment.
- Complete a school absence note.
- Write a formal complaint.
- Summarize what happened yesterday.

### Grammar Layer

Purpose: Explain the grammar needed for the communication goal.

Why it matters: Grammar becomes meaningful when learners see what it helps them do.

Design principles:

- Teach grammar after the situation and model language.
- Focus on one primary grammar pattern.
- Use examples from the lesson.
- Explain only what is needed for communication.
- Connect the pattern to the broader grammar map.

Examples:

- Modal verbs for asking for help.
- Perfekt for telling what happened.
- Dative pronouns for asking someone for help.
- weil clauses for giving reasons.
- Konjunktiv II for polite requests.

### Sentence Architecture Layer

Purpose: Show how the sentence is built and where the conjugated verb goes.

Why it matters: Learners need to connect ideas, not only know grammar pieces.

Design principles:

- Tag sentence type.
- Mark verb position.
- Identify connector type.
- Use simple word-order diagrams.
- Connect structure to function.

Examples:

- W-question: Wo ist die Apotheke?
- Modal verb bracket: Ich möchte einen Termin machen.
- Nebensatz: Ich kann nicht kommen, weil ich krank bin.
- Relative clause: Das ist der Kurs, der mir hilft.

### Retrieval Practice Layer

Purpose: Bring back previous language and connect it to the new lesson.

Why it matters: Retrieval turns isolated lessons into a growing system.

Design principles:

- Recall before rereading.
- Mix old and new language.
- Reuse earlier phrases in new situations.
- Keep review short and targeted.

Examples:

- Recall three ways to ask for help.
- Reuse an appointment phrase in a doctor context.
- Choose between weil, denn, and deshalb.
- Rebuild a Perfekt sentence from prompts.

### Real-World Challenge

Purpose: Transfer lesson language into life or a realistic simulation.

Why it matters: The system promises usable German, not only completed exercises.

Design principles:

- Make the challenge concrete.
- Include a low-pressure option.
- Include a real-world option when possible.
- Include a stretch option for confident learners.

Examples:

- Practice the Apotheke dialogue aloud.
- Ask one real question in a shop.
- Write a real or simulated appointment email.
- Explain a past event to a partner without reading.

### Confidence Milestone

Purpose: Make progress visible as capability.

Why it matters: Adult learners need proof that they are becoming more able to use German.

Design principles:

- Write milestones as can-do outcomes.
- Focus on real-life confidence, not abstract knowledge.
- Include a with-support and independent version when helpful.
- Tie the milestone to the communication goal.

Examples:

- I can ask for help at an Apotheke without translating every sentence first.
- I can write a short email explaining why I missed an appointment.
- I can give my opinion and one reason in a conversation.

### How Metadata Supports Lesson Generation

Metadata makes lessons easier to create, sequence, search, and reuse.

For lesson generation, metadata defines the constraints before content is written. It keeps the lesson aligned with the system architecture and prevents drift into generic textbook material.

For curriculum planning, metadata makes it possible to track:

- which real-life situations are covered
- which communication functions are practiced
- which grammar concepts have been introduced
- which sentence structures have been reinforced
- which vocabulary themes are repeated
- which confidence milestones learners are building toward
- how lessons progress across CEFR levels

The metadata should be completed before drafting the lesson.

## Standard Situation Template

Use this template when creating or updating situation files. A situation file is not a single lesson. It is a reusable context that can contain many lessons, dialogues, writing tasks, challenges, and links to related situations.

## Overview

Explain what the situation is, who participates, why it matters, and how it can deepen across levels.

## Communication Functions Practiced

List the main actions learners practice in the situation.

Examples:

- asking for help
- describing a problem
- giving reasons
- making requests
- clarifying next steps

## Key Language Patterns

Name the reusable grammar and sentence patterns learners will meet repeatedly.

Examples:

- W-questions
- modal verbs
- Perfekt
- weil clauses
- polite request frames

## Vocabulary In Context

Group vocabulary by real use inside the situation, not as an isolated list.

Examples:

- symptoms and medicine in an Apotheke situation
- grades, homework, and support language in an Elternabend situation
- documents, appointments, and forms in a Bürgeramt situation

## Practice Dialogues

Every situation should include short, practical dialogues that learners can reuse or adapt.

### Dialogue 1: Typical Situation

Use the most common version of the situation.

### Dialogue 2: Common Variation

Show a realistic variation, complication, or follow-up question.

### Dialogue 3: Real-World Challenge

Add slightly more pressure, uncertainty, emotion, or complexity while staying level-appropriate.

## Writing Task

Include a writing task when the situation naturally involves messages, notes, forms, emails, or preparation.

### Example

Provide a short model text or prompt that learners can adapt.

## Real-Life Challenge

Give learners a way to transfer the language beyond the lesson.

### Beginner

Use a low-pressure practice task.

### Intermediate

Use a realistic simulated or prepared task.

### Advanced

Use a real-world task or a more spontaneous challenge.

## Related Situations

List situations that reuse the same patterns, vocabulary, functions, or confidence goals.

Examples:

- Apotheke relates to doctor appointments, explaining symptoms, and asking for help.
- Elternabend relates to school emails, speaking with a teacher, and requesting support.
- Bürgeramt relates to forms, appointments, documents, and clarification questions.

## 5. Grammar Through Situations Matrix

Grammar should be introduced when it solves a communication problem.

| Grammar Concept | Situation | Communication Task | Typical CEFR Level |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Modal verbs | Asking for help at the Apotheke | Say what you need or ask what you can do | A1-A2 |
| weil clauses | Explaining why you missed an appointment | Give a reason | A2-B1 |
| Perfekt | Telling what happened yesterday | Describe a past event | A2 |
| Dative pronouns | Asking someone for help | Say who receives help or information | A2 |
| Accusative objects | Ordering food | Say what you want, need, buy, or order | A1 |
| Relative clauses | Describing a person | Identify or describe someone with more detail | B1 |
| Konjunktiv II | Polite requests and suggestions | Ask diplomatically or propose a solution | B1-B2 |
| Separable verbs | Daily routines and appointments | Talk about calling, shopping, getting up, bringing along | A1-A2 |
| Two-way prepositions | Housing and directions | Explain where something is or where it goes | A2 |
| Passive voice | Explaining a process or complaint | Describe what is done, repaired, delivered, or decided | B1-B2 |
| Adjective endings | Describing products, people, apartments | Add precise detail to nouns | B1 |
| Genitive | Formal writing and institutions | Show possession, relationship, or formal connection | B2-C1 |
| Infinitive clauses with zu | Making plans or suggestions | Express intention, difficulty, preference, or proposal | B1 |
| Comparative and superlative | Comparing options | Choose between services, apartments, jobs, products | A2-B1 |
| Reported speech | Reporting what someone said | Summarize conversations or information | B2-C1 |

Product rule: no grammar concept should be introduced without a communication task attached.

| Perfekt bracket | Storytelling and explanations | Say what happened | A2 |
| Separable verb bracket | Daily life and appointments | Describe common actions | A1-A2 |
| Hauptsatz + Nebensatz | Giving reasons | Connect action and reason | A2-B1 |
| Subordinate clauses with dass | Reporting information | Say what someone knows, says, thinks, or hopes | A2-B1 |
| Adverbial connectors | Telling a story | Sequence events and show consequence | A2-B1 |
| Relative clauses | Describing people and objects | Add identifying detail | B1 |

| Discourse markers | Discussions and opinions | Structure viewpoints and arguments | B2-C1 |

Product rule: sentence architecture should be visible whenever learners need to connect ideas.

## 7. Confidence Milestone Framework

Success should be measured by capability rather than grammar knowledge.

Bad milestone:

```text
I understand dative pronouns.
```

Good milestone:

```text
I can ask for help at an Apotheke without translating every sentence first.
```

Strong confidence milestones:

1. I can introduce myself in a new group without reading from a script.
2. I can order food and ask one follow-up question.
3. I can ask for help at an Apotheke and describe one simple symptom.
4. I can make a simple appointment by phone or message.
5. I can change an appointment and give a reason.
6. I can ask someone to repeat or explain something more slowly.
7. I can write a short message to a teacher or office.
8. I can explain a problem with my apartment and ask for help.
9. I can describe what happened yesterday in a clear order.
10. I can tell a short story about a trip or experience.
11. I can give my opinion and support it with one reason.
12. I can politely disagree without stopping the conversation.
13. I can write a clear B1-style email explaining a problem.
14. I can make a suggestion and explain why it might help.
15. I can compare two options and choose one.
16. I can participate in a simple workplace update.
17. I can ask clarifying questions in a meeting or class.
18. I can write a polite complaint and request a solution.
19. I can summarize a conversation and name the next step.
20. I can use German in a real situation even when I do not know every word.

Confidence milestones should be used in lesson metadata, lesson reflection, curriculum tracking, and learner progress maps.

## 8. Lesson Creation Workflow

Use this process to create a new lesson:

1. Choose situation.
   Select a real-life context where the learner needs German.

2. Define communication goal.
   Write one clear can-do outcome.

3. Identify communication functions.
   Choose the primary function and supporting functions.

4. Select required vocabulary.
   Choose only the words needed to perform the task.

5. Select phrase patterns.
   Add reusable chunks that help learners communicate early.

6. Select grammar needed.
   Choose the grammar that supports the goal.

7. Select sentence architecture needed.
   Identify sentence types, connector types, and verb position patterns.

8. Build speaking activities.
   Move from supported repetition to realistic role play.

9. Build writing activities.
   Create a written task that matches the situation.

10. Create retrieval practice.
    Bring back previous vocabulary, phrases, grammar, or sentence structures.

11. Define real-world challenge.
    Give the learner a realistic way to use or simulate the language.

12. Define confidence milestone.
    Write the capability the learner should feel after the lesson.

### Workflow Output

At the end of the workflow, the lesson should have:

- a situation
- a can-do goal
- function tags
- vocabulary
- phrase patterns
- grammar in context
- sentence architecture
- speaking practice
- writing practice
- retrieval practice
- a real-world challenge
- a confidence milestone

## 9. Product Principles

### How Adults Actually Learn Languages

Adults learn best when language is meaningful, usable, and connected to real life.

Product principles:

- Communication before perfection
- Pattern before rule
- Meaning before memorization
- Confidence before complexity
- Retrieval before review
- Real life before exercises
- Situations before grammar
- Use before mastery

These principles should shape every lesson decision.

Do not ask first:

```text
What grammar chapter should this lesson teach?
```

Ask first:

```text
What does the learner need to do in German?
```

Then design the vocabulary, phrases, grammar, sentence architecture, speaking, writing, retrieval, challenge, and confidence milestone around that need.

The goal is not to know German.

The goal is to live part of your life in German.
