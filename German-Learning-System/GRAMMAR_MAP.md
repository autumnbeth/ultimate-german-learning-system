# Grammar Map

## Purpose

This document defines German grammar as a connected learning system for adult learners. It is designed as an information architecture blueprint for future diagrams, knowledge maps, curriculum planning, and lesson sequencing.

The goal is not to teach every rule here. The goal is to show how the major grammar systems relate to one another, where they usually enter the learning path, and how they expand from A1 through C1.

## Verb System

### Foundation Verbs

The verbs haben, sein, and werden are foundational because they operate at three levels:

1. Meaning verbs: They express possession, identity, state, becoming, or change.
2. Auxiliary verbs: They help build major tense and voice structures.
3. System connectors: They link present meaning to past, future, passive, and hypothetical forms.

```text
haben
├── Lexical meaning: to have
├── Perfekt auxiliary for many verbs
├── Plusquamperfekt auxiliary for many verbs
└── Konjunktiv II form: hätte

sein
├── Lexical meaning: to be
├── Perfekt auxiliary for movement/change-of-state verbs
├── Plusquamperfekt auxiliary for movement/change-of-state verbs
├── State passive helper
└── Konjunktiv II form: wäre

werden
├── Lexical meaning: to become
├── Futur I auxiliary
├── Futur II auxiliary
├── Process passive auxiliary
└── Konjunktiv II form: würde
```

### How haben, sein, and werden Connect the Verb System

```text
Verb System
├── Präsens
│   ├── Uses the main verb directly
│   ├── ich habe, ich bin, ich werde
│   └── Foundation for present meaning and near-future plans
├── Perfekt
│   ├── haben + Partizip II
│   ├── sein + Partizip II
│   └── Used for most spoken past events
├── Präteritum
│   ├── Common for sein, haben, modal verbs
│   ├── Important in stories and written German
│   └── Expands into narrative control
├── Plusquamperfekt
│   ├── hatte + Partizip II
│   ├── war + Partizip II
│   └── Shows one past event before another past event
├── Futur I
│   ├── werden + Infinitiv
│   └── Used for future, prediction, or assumption
├── Futur II
│   ├── werden + Partizip II + haben/sein
│   └── Used for completed future or assumptions about the past
├── Passive Voice
│   ├── werden + Partizip II for process passive
│   └── sein + Partizip II for state passive
└── Konjunktiv
    ├── hätte for wishes, politeness, unreal conditions
    ├── wäre for wishes, politeness, unreal conditions
    └── würde + Infinitiv for many conditional forms
```

### Verb System by CEFR Level

| Level | Verb System Focus | Learner Capability |
| --- | --- | --- |
| A1 | Präsens, haben, sein, common verbs, first modal verbs | Say basic needs, identity, routines, and simple preferences |
| A2 | Perfekt, more modal verbs, separable verbs, reflexive verbs | Talk about past events, plans, obligations, and daily situations |
| B1 | Präteritum, Plusquamperfekt, werden, Konjunktiv II basics | Tell stories, make polite requests, describe sequences, express wishes |
| B2 | Passive voice, prepositional verbs, tense choice, reported speech awareness | Explain processes, discuss complex situations, adapt tone |
| C1 | Advanced tense, mood, passive alternatives, idiomatic verb patterns | Control nuance, register, implication, and professional/academic style |

## Sentence Structure System

Sentence structure is the backbone of German communication. Learners need to see word order as a system of patterns rather than a set of surprises.

### Visual Hierarchy

```text
Sentence Structure
├── Main Clauses
│   ├── Position 1: topic or subject
│   ├── Position 2: conjugated verb
│   └── End position: infinitive, participle, separable prefix
├── Yes/No Questions
│   ├── Position 1: conjugated verb
│   └── Subject follows the verb
├── W-Questions
│   ├── Position 1: question word
│   ├── Position 2: conjugated verb
│   └── Subject usually follows the verb
├── Modal Verbs
│   ├── Position 2: conjugated modal verb
│   └── End position: main verb infinitive
├── Separable Verbs
│   ├── Position 2: conjugated verb stem
│   └── End position: separable prefix
├── Subordinate Clauses
│   ├── Introduced by subordinating connector
│   └── Conjugated verb moves to the end
└── Relative Clauses
    ├── Introduced by relative pronoun
    ├── Relative pronoun has gender, number, and case
    └── Conjugated verb moves to the end
```

### Word Order Patterns

| Structure | Pattern | Example Function | Typical Introduction |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Main clause | Topic/subject + verb + rest | Make statements | A1 |
| Yes/no question | Verb + subject + rest | Ask if something is true | A1 |
| W-question | Question word + verb + subject + rest | Ask for specific information | A1 |
| Modal verb clause | Subject + modal verb + rest + infinitive | Say can, must, want, should | A1-A2 |
| Separable verb clause | Subject + verb stem + rest + prefix | Describe daily actions | A1-A2 |
| Perfekt clause | Subject + haben/sein + rest + participle | Talk about the past | A2 |
| Subordinate clause | Connector + subject + rest + verb | Give reason, condition, content | A2-B1 |
| Relative clause | Noun + relative pronoun + rest + verb | Add detail to a noun | B1 |
| Complex sentence | Main clause + subordinate/relative clauses | Explain, argue, narrate | B1-C1 |

### Sentence Structure Expansion

```text
A1: I can make simple sentences and questions.
├── Ich wohne in Berlin.
├── Wohnst du in Berlin?
└── Wo wohnst du?

A2: I can combine actions, plans, and reasons.
├── Ich muss morgen arbeiten.
├── Ich habe einen Termin gemacht.
└── Ich lerne Deutsch, weil ich in Deutschland wohne.

B1: I can connect ideas and add detail.
├── Ich suche eine Wohnung, die nicht zu teuer ist.
└── Ich konnte nicht kommen, obwohl ich Zeit hatte.

B2: I can structure complex explanations and arguments.
└── Obwohl die Lösung teuer ist, halte ich sie langfristig für sinnvoll.

C1: I can manage emphasis, register, and information flow.
└── Entscheidend ist nicht nur, was vorgeschlagen wurde, sondern wie realistisch die Umsetzung ist.
```

## Case System

Cases show the role of noun phrases in a sentence. For adult learners, the case system should be taught as a meaning and function map, not only as article tables.

### Case Relationship Map

```text
Cases
├── Nominativ
│   ├── Core role: subject
│   ├── Question: who or what is doing/being?
│   ├── First appears: A1
│   └── Foundation for articles, pronouns, and adjective endings
├── Akkusativ
│   ├── Core role: direct object
│   ├── Question: who or what receives the action directly?
│   ├── First appears: A1
│   └── Expands through verbs, prepositions, time expressions
├── Dativ
│   ├── Core role: indirect object, recipient, location with many prepositions
│   ├── Question: to whom, for whom, where?
│   ├── First appears: A2
│   └── Expands through dative verbs, prepositions, two-way prepositions
└── Genitiv
    ├── Core role: possession, relationship, formal connection
    ├── Question: whose?
    ├── First appears: B1 awareness, B2 active formal use
    └── Expands through formal writing, nominal style, academic language
```

### Case Learning Path

| Level | Case Focus | Learner Capability |
| --- | --- | --- |
| A1 | Nominativ and Akkusativ | Say who does something and what they need, buy, have, like, or see |
| A2 | Dativ with common verbs and prepositions | Talk about locations, recipients, help, appointments, and daily interactions |
| B1 | Case contrast and Genitiv awareness | Understand more complex noun phrases, relative pronouns, and formal written patterns |
| B2 | Genitiv and case precision in formal contexts | Read and write more precise formal, professional, and institutional German |
| C1 | Case as style and precision tool | Control complex noun phrases, register, and dense written expression |

### Case Connections

```text
Cases connect to:
├── Articles
│   └── der, den, dem, des show case signals
├── Pronouns
│   └── ich/mich/mir; er/ihn/ihm
├── Adjective Endings
│   └── endings depend on article, gender, number, and case
├── Prepositions
│   └── many prepositions require a specific case
├── Verbs
│   └── some verbs strongly expect accusative or dative complements
└── Relative Clauses
    └── relative pronoun case depends on its role inside the relative clause
```

## Major Systems as Map Nodes

### Sentence Structure

- Typical introduction: A1
- Prerequisites: Basic verbs, pronouns, simple vocabulary
- Related concepts: Verb position, questions, connectors, subordinate clauses
- Expansion: From simple main clauses to complex argument and information flow

### Verbs

- Typical introduction: A1
- Prerequisites: Pronouns, sentence meaning
- Related concepts: Tenses, auxiliaries, modals, separable verbs, reflexive verbs
- Expansion: From present-tense communication to nuanced tense, mood, and register

### Tenses

- Typical introduction: A1
- Prerequisites: Verb conjugation, auxiliary verbs
- Related concepts: haben, sein, werden, participles, word order
- Expansion: From Präsens to spoken past, narrative past, completed past, future, and assumptions

### Auxiliary Verbs

- Typical introduction: A1
- Prerequisites: Basic verb use
- Related concepts: Perfekt, Plusquamperfekt, Futur, Passive Voice, Konjunktiv
- Expansion: From meaning verbs to structural engines of the verb system

### Nouns

- Typical introduction: A1
- Prerequisites: Vocabulary
- Related concepts: Articles, gender, plural, cases, compounds
- Expansion: From concrete nouns to abstract, compound, and formal noun systems

### Articles

- Typical introduction: A1
- Prerequisites: Nouns and gender
- Related concepts: Cases, adjective endings, pronouns
- Expansion: From basic der/die/das to article-case-adjective systems

### Cases

- Typical introduction: A1
- Prerequisites: Nouns, articles, sentence roles
- Related concepts: Prepositions, pronouns, adjective endings, relative clauses
- Expansion: From subject/object contrast to formal precision and complex noun phrases

### Pronouns

- Typical introduction: A1
- Prerequisites: Person, number, sentence roles
- Related concepts: Cases, reflexive verbs, possessives, relative clauses
- Expansion: From basic personal reference to cohesion and discourse control

### Adjectives

- Typical introduction: A1
- Prerequisites: Nouns and simple descriptions
- Related concepts: Articles, cases, comparison, noun phrases
- Expansion: From simple descriptions to precise, case-marked, register-sensitive noun phrases

### Prepositions

- Typical introduction: A1
- Prerequisites: Nouns, places, time expressions
- Related concepts: Cases, verbs, location/direction, formal style
- Expansion: From common place/time phrases to idiomatic and abstract prepositional patterns

### Connectors

- Typical introduction: A1
- Prerequisites: Main clauses
- Related concepts: Subordinate clauses, word order, argumentation
- Expansion: From simple linking to argument, contrast, concession, cause, and discourse flow

### Relative Clauses

- Typical introduction: B1
- Prerequisites: Cases, pronouns, subordinate word order
- Related concepts: Noun detail, adjective-like information, sentence complexity
- Expansion: From identifying people/things to dense written precision

### Passive Voice

- Typical introduction: B1
- Prerequisites: werden, participles, tense system
- Related concepts: Formal writing, process description, state passive, passive alternatives
- Expansion: From basic process descriptions to formal, institutional, and academic style

## Design Implication

Future lessons, diagrams, and knowledge maps should avoid presenting grammar as isolated chapters. Each grammar concept should be tagged by:

- Communication function
- CEFR level
- Prerequisites
- Related concepts
- Example real-life situations
- Spoken use
- Written use
- Review links to earlier concepts

This makes grammar visible as a connected system that supports the central promise of German Learning System: turning scattered knowledge into usable communication.
