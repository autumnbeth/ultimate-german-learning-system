# Foundation Reference

## Purpose

`FOUNDATION_REFERENCE.md` is the learner's map of the German language system.

It is not a curriculum, a syllabus, or an ordered learning sequence. Learners do not need to study this file from top to bottom. They use it as a reference guide when they meet patterns in lessons, situations, conversations, writing tasks, or real life.

The Foundation Reference answers:

```text
How does German work?
```

Situational lessons answer:

```text
How do I use German?
```

This document organizes German around five major systems:

1. Sentence Architecture
2. Verb System
3. Connector System
4. Noun System
5. Communication Building Blocks

Each system helps learners connect scattered grammar knowledge into a usable mental model.

## Foundation Verb Map

The verbs `haben`, `sein`, and `werden` are system verbs. They are ordinary verbs, but they also build major grammar structures.

```text
haben
├── lexical verb: Ich habe einen Termin.
├── Perfekt auxiliary: Ich habe einen Termin gemacht.
├── Plusquamperfekt auxiliary: Ich hatte den Termin gemacht.
└── Konjunktiv II: Ich hätte eine Frage.

sein
├── lexical verb: Ich bin krank.
├── Perfekt auxiliary for movement/change: Ich bin gegangen.
├── Plusquamperfekt auxiliary: Ich war gegangen.
├── state passive helper: Die Tür ist geschlossen.
└── Konjunktiv II: Ich wäre gern dabei.

werden
├── lexical verb: Es wird besser.
├── Futur I: Ich werde morgen anrufen.
├── Futur II: Ich werde es gemacht haben.
├── process passive: Das Formular wird geprüft.
└── Konjunktiv II helper: Ich würde gern kommen.
```

Why these matter:

- `haben` opens spoken past for most verbs.
- `sein` opens identity, state, movement, change, and some passive structures.
- `werden` opens future meaning, passive voice, and polite/hypothetical language.
- Together, they connect everyday speech to advanced written and formal German.

## Sentence Architecture Control Panel

German word order becomes easier when learners identify the sentence type first.

| Sentence Type | Trigger | Verb Position | Example | Communication Use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Hauptsatz | independent idea | position 2 | Ich brauche einen Termin. | stating, answering, explaining simply |
| Yes-no question | confirmation needed | position 1 | Haben Sie heute Zeit? | checking, confirming, asking for help |
| W-question | information needed | question word + position 2 | Wann haben Sie Zeit? | asking for details |
| Modal sentence | modal verb | modal in position 2, infinitive at end | Ich muss morgen arbeiten. | needs, obligations, advice |
| Separable verb | separable prefix | stem in position 2, prefix at end | Ich rufe morgen an. | daily actions, appointments |
| Perfekt sentence | spoken past | haben/sein in position 2, participle at end | Ich habe angerufen. | stories, updates, explanations |
| Nebensatz | subordinating connector | verb at end | weil ich krank bin | reasons, conditions, reports |
| Relative clause | relative pronoun | verb at end | der mir hilft | describing and identifying |

## Case System Map

Cases are not just endings. They are role signals.

```text
Nominativ
├── Who or what is the subject?
├── First appears: A1
└── Example: Der Termin ist morgen.

Akkusativ
├── Who or what receives the direct action?
├── First appears: A1
└── Example: Ich brauche den Termin.

Dativ
├── To whom, for whom, where, or with whom?
├── First appears: A2
└── Example: Ich helfe dem Kind.

Genitiv
├── Whose? Of what?
├── First appears: B1 awareness, B2 formal control
└── Example: die Adresse des Arztes
```

The case system connects to:

- articles: der, den, dem, des
- pronouns: ich, mich, mir
- prepositions: mit + Dativ, für + Akkusativ
- adjective endings: ein guter Termin, einen guten Termin
- relative clauses: der Mann, dem ich helfe

## Connector Control Panel

Connectors are grammar switches. They do not only add meaning; they often choose the sentence architecture.

| Connector Type | Examples | Word Order Effect | Communication Function |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Coordinating | und, aber, oder, denn, sondern | main-clause order stays stable | add, contrast, choose, give simple reasons |
| Subordinating | weil, dass, wenn, obwohl, damit | conjugated verb goes to the end | explain, report, condition, concede, give purpose |
| Adverbial | deshalb, trotzdem, danach, zuerst, außerdem | connector often takes position 1, verb stays position 2 | show result, contrast, sequence, organization |
| Discourse markers | meiner Meinung nach, zum Beispiel, einerseits, andererseits | organize flow; often phrase-level or position-1 patterns | express opinion, give examples, structure arguments |

Core contrast:

```text
denn     -> main clause + main clause
Ich lerne Deutsch, denn ich wohne in Deutschland.

weil     -> main clause + subordinate clause
Ich lerne Deutsch, weil ich in Deutschland wohne.

deshalb  -> sentence adverb + verb-second main clause
Ich wohne in Deutschland. Deshalb lerne ich Deutsch.
```

## A1-B1 Complete Grammar Structure Map

This section turns the foundation into a structure map for A1 through B1. It is not a lesson order. It is a reference model that shows which parts of German remain stable while new layers are added.

Core principle:

```text
Auxiliary engine
→ sentence frame
→ tense stack
→ clause control
→ passive system
→ mood layer
→ modal stacking
→ B1 expression layer
```

### Level 1: The Engine - Auxiliary Verbs

The first engine of German is `haben`, `sein`, and `werden`.

These three verbs must become automatic because they build past, future, passive, conditional, and modal-like structures.

| Verb | Core Meaning | System Function | Key Forms |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| haben | to have | Perfekt with most verbs; Plusquamperfekt; Konjunktiv II with hätte | ich habe, du hast, er/sie/es hat, wir haben |
| sein | to be | Perfekt with movement/change verbs; Plusquamperfekt; Zustandspassiv; Konjunktiv II with wäre | ich bin, du bist, er/sie/es ist, wir sind |
| werden | to become | Futur I; Futur II; Vorgangspassiv; Konjunktiv II with würde | ich werde, du wirst, er/sie/es wird, wir werden |

Reference:

```text
Perfekt with haben:
Ich habe gelernt.

Perfekt with sein:
Ich bin gegangen.

Future/passive with werden:
Ich werde anrufen.
Das Formular wird geprüft.
```

### Level 2: The Frame Never Changes

German sentence architecture is stable. New grammar layers are added into the same frame.

#### Hauptsatz Frame

```text
Position 1  +  Verb in position 2  +  middle field  +  verb element at the end

Ich             habe                    gestern Deutsch     gelernt.
Ich             werde                   morgen Deutsch      lernen.
```

Rule:

```text
One conjugated verb only = always position 2.
Extra verb pieces go to the end.
```

#### Nebensatz Frame

```text
Connector  +  subject  +  middle field  +  all verb elements at the end

weil          ich        Deutsch            lerne
weil          ich        Deutsch            gelernt habe
weil          ich        Deutsch            lernen möchte
```

Rule:

```text
Nebensatz = comma + connector + verb at the end.
```

### Level 3: The Tense Stack

German tenses follow one structural idea:

```text
Only one conjugated verb appears early.
Everything else stacks at the end.
```

| Tense | Structure | Example | What Changes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Präsens | conjugated verb only | Ich lerne. | one verb |
| Perfekt | haben/sein + Partizip II | Ich habe gelernt. | auxiliary in position 2; participle at end |
| Präteritum | past stem form | Ich lernte. / Ich war. | one past verb, common with sein/haben/modals |
| Plusquamperfekt | hatte/war + Partizip II | Ich hatte gelernt. | past auxiliary + participle |
| Futur I | werden + Infinitiv | Ich werde lernen. | werden in position 2; infinitive at end |
| Futur II | werden + Partizip II + haben/sein | Ich werde gelernt haben. | future frame around completed action |

Reference ladder:

```text
Präsens:          Ich lerne.
Perfekt:          Ich habe gelernt.
Präteritum:       Ich lernte. / Ich war.
Plusquamperfekt:  Ich hatte gelernt.
Futur I:          Ich werde lernen.
Futur II:         Ich werde gelernt haben.
```

### Auxiliary Choice: haben vs sein

| Auxiliary | Usually With | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| sein | motion, change of state, a small set of core verbs | gehen, kommen, fahren, werden, aufwachen, sterben |
| haben | most other verbs | lernen, essen, arbeiten, schreiben, kaufen, sehen |

Reference:

```text
Ich bin nach Hause gegangen.
Ich habe einen Brief geschrieben.
```

### Block A: Clause Control

Clause control explains which words control the structure of the sentence.

#### Konjunktionen: Verb To The End

These connectors introduce a subordinate clause.

| Connector | Function | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| weil | because | Ich übe, weil ich besser sprechen möchte. |
| dass | that | Ich glaube, dass der Termin morgen ist. |
| obwohl | although | Ich gehe, obwohl ich müde bin. |
| wenn | if/when, repeated | Wenn ich Zeit habe, rufe ich an. |
| als | when, one past moment | Als ich klein war, ... |
| nachdem | after | Nachdem ich gegessen hatte, ... |
| sobald | as soon as | Sobald ich Zeit habe, melde ich mich. |
| sodass | so that/as consequence | Es war laut, sodass wir nichts gehört haben. |
| damit | so that, purpose | Ich lerne, damit ich sicherer spreche. |

Pattern:

```text
Connector + subject + middle field + verb at end
weil        ich       Deutsch       lerne
```

#### Konnektoren: Verb Stays Position 2

These connectors often occupy position 1 in a main clause. The conjugated verb stays in position 2.

| Connector | Function | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| also | so/therefore | Also bleibe ich zu Hause. |
| deshalb | that is why | Deshalb schlafe ich früher. |
| trotzdem | nevertheless | Trotzdem gehen wir. |
| außerdem | moreover | Außerdem brauche ich eine Kopie. |

Pattern:

```text
Connector + verb + subject + rest
Deshalb     lerne   ich       Deutsch.
```

#### Relativsatz

Relative clauses add information about a noun. The relative pronoun agrees with the noun in gender and number, but its case comes from its role inside the relative clause.

| Gender | Nominativ | Akkusativ | Dativ |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| der | der | den | dem |
| die | die | die | der |
| das | das | das | dem |
| plural | die | die | denen |

Examples:

```text
Der Mann, der dort sitzt, ist mein Lehrer.
Das Formular, das ich brauche, liegt hier.
Die Frau, der ich geholfen habe, ist nett.
```

#### Finalsatz: Purpose

German uses two common purpose structures.

| Structure | Use | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| damit + subject + verb at end | different subject or explicit subject | Ich lerne, damit er mich versteht. |
| um ... zu + infinitive | same subject | Ich lerne, um zu bestehen. |

### Block B: Passive System

Passive is not a separate world. It uses the same tense ladder and the same verb-position rules.

#### Vorgangspassiv: Process Passive

Formula:

```text
werden + Partizip II
```

| Tense | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Präsens | Das Haus wird gebaut. |
| Präteritum | Das Haus wurde gebaut. |
| Perfekt | Das Haus ist gebaut worden. |
| Plusquamperfekt | Das Haus war gebaut worden. |
| Futur I | Das Haus wird gebaut werden. |

Meaning:

```text
Object becomes subject in passive.
The focus is on the action or process.
```

#### Passiv With Modal Verb

Pattern:

```text
Modal in position 2 + Partizip II + werden at the end
```

Examples:

```text
Das Haus muss gebaut werden.
Das Formular kann geprüft werden.
Der Termin soll bestätigt werden.
```

Rule:

```text
werden stays before the final modal-like infinitive stack.
```

#### Zustandspassiv: State Passive

Formula:

```text
sein + Partizip II
```

Examples:

```text
Die Tür ist geschlossen.
Das Haus war gebaut.
```

Meaning:

```text
Vorgangspassiv = action happening
Zustandspassiv = result or state
```

#### lassen + Infinitiv

`lassen + Infinitiv` often creates a passive-adjacent meaning.

| Meaning | Example |
| --- | --- |
| I have something done | Ich lasse mein Auto reparieren. |
| something can be done/explained | Das lässt sich erklären. |

Related structure:

```text
haben + Infinitiv + Modal

Ich habe kommen müssen.
Ich habe arbeiten sollen.
```

Rule:

```text
No Partizip II here. The infinitive stack stays infinitive.
```

### Block C: Konjunktiv System

Konjunktiv is a mood layer, not a new time layer.

It changes stance:

- hypothetical
- conditional
- polite
- unreal
- reported
- journalistic or formal distance

#### Konjunktiv II: Hypothetical / Conditional

Most common spoken form:

```text
würde + Infinitiv

Ich würde gern kommen.
Er würde das kaufen.
```

Common strong forms:

| Infinitive | Konjunktiv II |
| --- | --- |
| sein | wäre |
| haben | hätte |
| können | könnte |
| müssen | müsste |
| werden | würde |
| wollen | wollte |

Examples:

```text
Ich hätte gern einen Termin.
Es wäre gut, wenn wir früher anfangen.
Könnten Sie mir helfen?
```

Past hypothetical:

```text
hätte/wäre + Partizip II

Ich hätte das gemacht.
Er wäre gegangen.
```

#### Konjunktiv I: Reported Speech

Konjunktiv I reports what someone says while creating distance.

Common pattern:

```text
Er sagt, er sei krank.
Sie meint, sie habe keine Zeit.
Er behauptet, er komme später.
```

Quick reference:

| Verb | Konjunktiv I Signal |
| --- | --- |
| sein | er sei |
| haben | er habe |
| kommen | er komme |
| gehen | er gehe |

### Block D: Modal Stacking

Modal stacking means a modal combines with another verb layer.

The foundation is always the same:

```text
Modal in position 2.
Everything else goes to the end.
```

| Construction | Formula | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Präsens + modal | Modal + Infinitiv | Ich kann kommen. |
| Perfekt + modal | haben + Infinitiv + Modal | Ich habe kommen können. |
| Plusquamperfekt + modal | hatte + Infinitiv + Modal | Ich hatte kommen können. |
| Futur + modal | werden + Infinitiv + Modal | Ich werde kommen müssen. |
| Passive + modal | Modal + Partizip II + werden | Es muss gemacht werden. |
| Konjunktiv II + modal | könnte/müsste/sollte + Infinitiv | Das könnte sein. |

Modal quick reference:

| Modal | Meaning | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| müssen | must, have to | Ich muss arbeiten. |
| können | can, be able to | Ich kann schwimmen. |
| dürfen | may, be allowed to | Du darfst hier parken. |
| wollen | want to | Ich will reisen. |
| sollen | should, be supposed to | Du sollst das machen. |
| mögen/möchten | like / would like | Ich möchte einen Kaffee. |

### B1 Layer: Time Logic And Expression

B1 adds nuance. The grammar is still built on the same structure, but learners can now show time relationships, sequence, consequence, degree, and stance more clearly.

#### Plusquamperfekt: Time Anchor

Use Plusquamperfekt to show one past event before another past event.

```text
Nachdem ich gegessen hatte, ging ich spazieren.
```

Map:

```text
Past event 1: hatte gegessen
Past event 2: ging spazieren
```

#### Temporal Connectors

| Connector | Meaning | Time Logic |
| --- | --- | --- |
| nachdem | after | earlier action first |
| sobald | as soon as | immediate sequence |
| als | when | one past moment |
| wenn | when/if | repeated, future, or conditional |

#### Konsekutiv: Consequence

| Connector | Structure | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| sodass | Nebensatz with verb at end | Es war kalt, sodass wir zu Hause blieben. |
| also | Hauptsatz connector | Es war kalt. Also blieben wir zu Hause. |

#### Gradual Meaning

Useful expression markers:

```text
sehr
besonders
extrem
völlig
total
ziemlich
ganz
eher
kaum
```

#### Redepartikeln

These are emotional or conversational particles. They are common in spoken German and should be recognized before learners try to overuse them.

```text
doch
ja
wohl
eben
halt
```

#### Futur I: Prognosis, Promise, Assumption

Formula:

```text
werden + Infinitiv
```

Uses:

```text
Ich werde kommen.       -> promise
Er wird es schaffen.    -> prediction or encouragement
Er wird zu Hause sein.  -> assumption
```

In speech, Präsens is often preferred for practical future plans:

```text
Morgen rufe ich an.
Nächste Woche komme ich vorbei.
```

#### Prognosis And Assumption Patterns

```text
Ich vermute, dass ...
Wahrscheinlich wird ...
Es könnte sein, dass ...
Es scheint, als ob ...
```

Reminder:

```text
dass + conjugated verb at the end
```

## 1. Sentence Architecture

Sentence architecture explains how German sentences are built, how ideas connect, and where the conjugated verb goes.

### Hauptsatz

Purpose:

A Hauptsatz is a main clause. It can stand alone and carry a complete idea.

Why it matters:

The Hauptsatz is the foundation for statements, answers, updates, descriptions, and many connected ideas. Learners need it for almost every situation.

Common learner difficulties:

- Forgetting that the conjugated verb belongs in position 2.
- Thinking the subject must always come first.
- Moving the verb when a time phrase or connector begins the sentence.
- Building sentences word-by-word from English.

Related situations:

- Introducing yourself
- Ordering food
- Talking about daily life
- Giving updates at work
- Describing a problem

Related communication functions:

- stating facts
- describing needs
- answering questions
- giving updates
- explaining simple problems

Reference pattern:

```text
Position 1 + conjugated verb + middle field + end field

Ich        lerne              jeden Tag      Deutsch.
Morgen     lerne              ich            Deutsch.
```

### Nebensatz

Purpose:

A Nebensatz is a subordinate clause. It depends on another clause and usually sends the conjugated verb to the end.

Why it matters:

Nebensätze let learners give reasons, explain conditions, report information, add context, and connect ideas. They are essential for emails, opinions, apologies, explanations, and exams.

Common learner difficulties:

- Keeping the verb in position 2 after weil, dass, or wenn.
- Forgetting that the conjugated verb moves to the end.
- Creating very long subordinate clauses before the basic pattern feels stable.
- Confusing weil with denn or deshalb.

Related situations:

- Explaining why you missed an appointment
- Writing a school email
- Making a complaint
- TELC B1 writing
- Discussing concerns at an Elternabend

Related communication functions:

- giving reasons
- explaining problems
- apologizing
- reporting information
- expressing opinions

Reference pattern:

```text
Connector + subject + middle field + conjugated verb at the end

weil      ich       heute arbeiten             muss
dass      sie       morgen einen Termin        hat
wenn      ich       Zeit                       habe
```

### Questions

Purpose:

Questions allow learners to get information, confirm understanding, ask for help, and participate in conversation.

Why it matters:

A learner who can ask questions can keep communication alive even with limited vocabulary.

Common learner difficulties:

- Mixing yes-no question word order with W-question word order.
- Forgetting that yes-no questions begin with the conjugated verb.
- Forgetting that W-questions keep the conjugated verb in position 2.
- Not having repair questions ready when communication breaks down.

Related situations:

- Apotheke
- Doctor appointment
- Public transport
- Government office
- First day at work
- School meeting

Related communication functions:

- asking for help
- asking for information
- asking for repetition
- clarifying
- confirming plans

Reference patterns:

```text
Yes-no question:
Conjugated verb + subject + rest
Haben Sie heute Zeit?

W-question:
Question word + conjugated verb + subject + rest
Wann haben Sie Zeit?
```

### Relative Clauses

Purpose:

Relative clauses add information about a noun: a person, object, place, idea, or option.

Why it matters:

They help learners describe and identify things more precisely without starting a new sentence every time.

Common learner difficulties:

- Choosing the correct relative pronoun.
- Understanding that the relative pronoun has gender, number, and case.
- Remembering verb-final word order.
- Making the clause too long before the basic pattern is stable.

Related situations:

- Describing an apartment
- Describing a person
- Talking about products
- Explaining which document is needed
- Discussing work responsibilities

Related communication functions:

- describing
- identifying
- clarifying
- comparing options
- expressing preferences

Reference pattern:

```text
Noun + relative pronoun + middle field + conjugated verb at the end

Das ist der Kurs, der mir hilft.
Ich suche eine Wohnung, die nicht zu teuer ist.
Das ist das Formular, das ich brauche.
```

### Information Flow

Purpose:

Information flow explains how German uses position 1 to guide attention while keeping the conjugated verb anchored in position 2.

Why it matters:

Learners often think German word order is random. Information flow shows that German can move time, place, contrast, result, or topic to the front while keeping a stable verb anchor.

Common learner difficulties:

- Treating position 1 as always the subject.
- Forgetting subject-verb inversion after time phrases or adverbial connectors.
- Overusing English-style order.
- Not knowing how to emphasize what matters.

Related situations:

- Telling stories
- Giving updates
- Writing emails
- Explaining problems
- Participating in meetings

Related communication functions:

- sequencing events
- emphasizing
- contrasting
- summarizing
- giving updates

Reference pattern:

```text
Chosen starting point + conjugated verb + subject + rest

Heute                 habe              ich      einen Termin.
Deshalb               lerne             ich      Deutsch.
Im Alltag             brauche           ich      mehr Sicherheit.
```

## 2. Verb System

The verb system explains how German expresses action, time, possibility, obligation, plans, and sentence structure.

### Present Tense

Purpose:

The present tense expresses current actions, general truths, routines, and often future plans.

Why it matters:

It is the base tense for daily communication. Learners can say a lot with the present tense before they master the full tense system.

Common learner difficulties:

- Forgetting verb endings.
- Overusing infinitives.
- Translating English continuous forms directly.
- Not realizing present tense can express future meaning with a time phrase.

Related situations:

- Introducing yourself
- Daily routines
- Work updates
- Making plans
- Small talk

Related communication functions:

- describing routines
- stating facts
- talking about yourself
- making plans
- giving updates

Reference patterns:

```text
Ich wohne in Berlin.
Sie arbeitet heute.
Morgen gehe ich zum Arzt.
```

### Modal Verbs

Purpose:

Modal verbs express ability, necessity, permission, desire, obligation, advice, and polite intention.

Why it matters:

Modal verbs unlock high-value communication early: I can, I need to, I would like to, should I, may I, can you.

Common learner difficulties:

- Forgetting that the modal verb is conjugated and the main verb goes to the end.
- Confusing möchte, will, soll, muss, darf, and kann.
- Using modal verbs without a clear infinitive.
- Forgetting subject-verb changes: ich kann, du kannst, er kann.

Related situations:

- Apotheke
- Restaurant
- Work tasks
- Appointments
- Asking for help
- Giving advice

Related communication functions:

- asking for help
- making requests
- expressing needs
- giving advice
- making suggestions

Reference pattern:

```text
Subject + modal verb + middle field + infinitive at the end

Ich     möchte       einen Termin        machen.
Wir     müssen       morgen früh         arbeiten.
Kann    ich          Ihnen               helfen?
```

### Perfekt

Purpose:

Perfekt expresses many spoken past events.

Why it matters:

Learners need Perfekt to tell what happened, explain why they missed something, describe experiences, report problems, and make social conversation.

Common learner difficulties:

- Choosing haben or sein.
- Remembering the participle.
- Forgetting that the participle usually goes at the end.
- Mixing Perfekt word order with English past tense.

Related situations:

- Talking about your weekend
- Explaining a missed appointment
- Reporting an accident
- Giving a workplace update
- Telling a travel story

Related communication functions:

- telling stories
- explaining problems
- giving updates
- describing experiences
- apologizing

Reference pattern:

```text
Subject + haben/sein + middle field + Partizip II at the end

Ich     habe          gestern einen Termin       gemacht.
Sie     ist           nach Berlin                gefahren.
```

### Future Meaning

Purpose:

German can express future meaning with the present tense plus time context, or with werden + infinitive.

Why it matters:

Future meaning supports appointments, plans, goals, promises, predictions, and next steps.

Common learner difficulties:

- Assuming every future idea needs werden.
- Forgetting that time phrases can make present tense future.
- Confusing werden as "become" with werden as a future auxiliary.
- Forgetting the infinitive at the end with werden.

Related situations:

- Making appointments
- Weekend plans
- Work planning
- Travel booking
- Reaching agreement

Related communication functions:

- talking about future plans
- making suggestions
- scheduling
- reaching agreement
- making commitments

Reference patterns:

```text
Present tense with future meaning:
Morgen rufe ich an.

werden + infinitive:
Ich werde morgen anrufen.
```

### Verb Brackets

Purpose:

Verb brackets explain why German often places one verb element early and another at the end.

Why it matters:

The verb bracket is one of the structures that makes German feel German. It connects modal verbs, separable verbs, Perfekt, future forms, and many complex sentences.

Common learner difficulties:

- Losing track of the end-field verb.
- Forgetting separable prefixes.
- Putting the infinitive or participle too early.
- Not recognizing that the sentence is "open" until the final verb element appears.

Related situations:

- Daily routines
- Appointments
- Storytelling
- Work updates
- Instructions

Related communication functions:

- describing actions
- making requests
- telling past events
- explaining plans
- understanding instructions

Reference patterns:

```text
Modal:
Ich muss morgen arbeiten.

Perfekt:
Ich habe gestern gearbeitet.

Separable verb:
Ich rufe dich morgen an.
```

## 3. Connector System

Connectors explain how German links ideas: addition, reason, contrast, condition, time, sequence, result, and argument.

### Coordinating Connectors

Purpose:

Coordinating connectors connect equal sentence parts or main clauses.

Why it matters:

They help learners move beyond one-sentence communication without changing the basic main-clause architecture.

Common learner difficulties:

- Treating all connectors as if they cause verb-final word order.
- Confusing denn and weil.
- Overusing und when contrast, reason, or correction is needed.

Related situations:

- Small talk
- Simple emails
- Explaining preferences
- Ordering and choosing
- Describing daily life

Related communication functions:

- adding information
- contrasting
- choosing
- giving simple reasons
- correcting

Reference examples:

```text
und      Ich trinke Kaffee und ich esse Kuchen.
aber     Ich möchte kommen, aber ich habe keine Zeit.
oder     Möchtest du Tee oder Kaffee?
denn     Ich lerne Deutsch, denn ich wohne in Deutschland.
sondern  Ich trinke keinen Kaffee, sondern Tee.
```

### Subordinating Connectors

Purpose:

Subordinating connectors introduce dependent clauses and send the conjugated verb to the end.

Why it matters:

They let learners explain reasons, conditions, content, time relationships, purpose, and contrast.

Common learner difficulties:

- Forgetting verb-final word order.
- Confusing wenn and wann.
- Confusing weil, denn, and deshalb.
- Beginning too many sentences with subordinate clauses before the pattern is stable.

Related situations:

- Appointment changes
- School emails
- Complaints
- TELC writing
- Explaining problems

Related communication functions:

- giving reasons
- explaining conditions
- reporting information
- apologizing
- giving purpose

Reference examples:

```text
weil     Ich lerne Deutsch, weil ich in Hamburg wohne.
dass     Ich glaube, dass der Termin morgen ist.
wenn     Wenn ich Zeit habe, rufe ich dich an.
obwohl   Ich lerne weiter, obwohl es schwierig ist.
damit    Ich uebe jeden Tag, damit ich sicherer spreche.
```

### Adverbial Connectors

Purpose:

Adverbial connectors act like sentence adverbs. They often stand in position 1, and the conjugated verb stays in position 2.

Why it matters:

They help learners show result, contrast, sequence, and organization while keeping main-clause word order.

Common learner difficulties:

- Treating deshalb, trotzdem, dann, and danach like subordinating connectors.
- Forgetting that the verb comes immediately after an adverbial connector in position 1.
- Confusing trotzdem with obwohl.

Related situations:

- Telling stories
- Explaining consequences
- Giving updates
- Writing emails
- Participating in discussions

Related communication functions:

- sequencing
- showing result
- contrasting
- organizing information
- summarizing

Reference examples:

```text
deshalb   Ich bin krank. Deshalb bleibe ich zu Hause.
trotzdem  Es regnet. Trotzdem gehe ich spazieren.
danach    Danach rufe ich dich an.
zuerst    Zuerst lesen wir den Text.
```

### Discourse Markers

Purpose:

Discourse markers organize speaking and writing at the level of ideas, stance, and argument.

Why it matters:

They help learners sound more coherent, especially in opinions, discussions, presentations, emails, and exams.

Common learner difficulties:

- Using advanced markers without enough sentence control.
- Overusing direct translations from English.
- Not knowing which markers are formal, conversational, or argumentative.

Related situations:

- TELC speaking
- Meetings
- Presentations
- Feedback conversations
- Opinion discussions

Related communication functions:

- expressing opinions
- giving examples
- contrasting ideas
- summarizing
- structuring arguments

Reference examples:

```text
meiner Meinung nach
zum Beispiel
ehrlich gesagt
einerseits
andererseits
zusammenfassend
```

## 4. Noun System

The noun system explains how German marks gender, role, reference, description, and relationships inside noun phrases.

### Articles

Purpose:

Articles mark noun gender, number, definiteness, and often case.

Why it matters:

Articles are one of the main signals German uses to show how nouns function in a sentence.

Common learner difficulties:

- Remembering gender.
- Seeing articles as random rather than informational.
- Forgetting case changes: der, den, dem, des.
- Avoiding articles when German requires them.

Related situations:

- Shopping
- Ordering food
- Describing housing
- Filling forms
- Talking about family

Related communication functions:

- identifying objects
- buying something
- describing people or things
- asking for information
- comparing options

Reference examples:

```text
der Termin
die Apotheke
das Formular
ein Termin
eine Frage
kein Problem
```

### Cases

Purpose:

Cases show the role of a noun phrase in a sentence.

Why it matters:

Cases help learners understand who is doing something, what receives the action, who receives help or information, and how nouns relate to each other.

Common learner difficulties:

- Trying to memorize case tables without meaning.
- Confusing accusative and dative.
- Not noticing that prepositions often control case.
- Forgetting pronoun case forms like mich, mir, ihn, ihm.

Related situations:

- Asking for help
- Giving someone information
- Buying or ordering
- Talking about locations
- Writing formal emails

Related communication functions:

- asking for help
- making requests
- giving information
- describing location
- explaining relationships

Reference map:

```text
Nominativ  -> subject
Akkusativ  -> direct object
Dativ      -> indirect object, recipient, many locations
Genitiv    -> possession, relationship, formal connection
```

### Pronouns

Purpose:

Pronouns replace or point back to people, objects, ideas, and relationships.

Why it matters:

Pronouns make communication faster and more natural. They also reveal case and sentence roles.

Common learner difficulties:

- Confusing subject and object forms: ich/mich/mir.
- Forgetting polite Sie.
- Losing track of what pronouns refer to.
- Confusing possessive articles with pronouns.

Related situations:

- Asking for help
- Talking with teachers or offices
- Discussing family
- Writing emails
- Telling stories

Related communication functions:

- referring back
- asking for help
- explaining problems
- telling stories
- managing conversations

Reference examples:

```text
ich / mich / mir
du / dich / dir
er / ihn / ihm
sie / sie / ihr
Sie / Sie / Ihnen
```

### Adjectives

Purpose:

Adjectives describe qualities, compare options, and make noun phrases more precise.

Why it matters:

Learners need adjectives to describe symptoms, apartments, products, people, experiences, preferences, and opinions.

Common learner difficulties:

- Understanding why adjective endings change.
- Knowing when adjectives need endings and when they do not.
- Comparing things naturally.
- Building long noun phrases without losing clarity.

Related situations:

- Apartment viewing
- Shopping
- Doctor appointment
- Describing people
- Comparing services

Related communication functions:

- describing
- comparing
- expressing preferences
- giving opinions
- clarifying

Reference examples:

```text
Das Zimmer ist groß.
Ich suche eine kleine Wohnung.
Diese Option ist besser.
Das ist der wichtigste Punkt.
```

## 5. Communication Building Blocks

Communication building blocks connect grammar and sentence architecture to what learners can do in German.

### Asking Questions

Purpose:

Asking questions helps learners get information, ask for help, confirm understanding, and keep conversations moving.

Why it matters:

Questions give learners agency. They can participate even when they do not know everything.

Common learner difficulties:

- Choosing between yes-no questions and W-questions.
- Forgetting verb position.
- Not having enough repair phrases.
- Asking questions too indirectly too early.

Related situations:

- Apotheke
- Public transport
- School
- Work
- Government office

Related communication functions:

- asking for help
- asking for information
- clarifying
- confirming
- managing conversations

Useful patterns:

```text
Können Sie mir helfen?
Was bedeutet das?
Wann ist der Termin?
Wo finde ich...?
```

### Giving Reasons

Purpose:

Giving reasons explains why something happens, why something matters, or why the learner needs something.

Why it matters:

Reasons connect facts into meaningful communication. They are essential for apologies, opinions, complaints, school emails, and exams.

Common learner difficulties:

- Confusing weil, denn, deshalb, and da.
- Forgetting verb-final word order after weil and da.
- Giving too much information before the basic structure is stable.

Related situations:

- Missed appointments
- TELC writing
- Complaints
- Elternabend
- Work meetings

Related communication functions:

- explaining
- apologizing
- supporting opinions
- requesting help
- justifying decisions

Useful patterns:

```text
Ich kann nicht kommen, weil ich krank bin.
Ich lerne Deutsch, denn ich wohne in Deutschland.
Ich bin krank. Deshalb bleibe ich zu Hause.
```

### Expressing Opinions

Purpose:

Expressing opinions lets learners say what they think, prefer, value, or recommend.

Why it matters:

Opinions move learners beyond survival communication into social, professional, academic, and civic participation.

Common learner difficulties:

- Giving an opinion without a reason.
- Overusing "ich denke" for every opinion.
- Not softening disagreement in polite contexts.
- Not connecting opinion language to examples.

Related situations:

- TELC speaking
- Workplace discussions
- Feedback conversations
- Social conversations
- Education and school meetings

Related communication functions:

- giving reasons
- agreeing
- disagreeing
- comparing options
- giving examples

Useful patterns:

```text
Ich finde das wichtig.
Meiner Meinung nach ist...
Aus meiner Sicht...
Ich sehe das anders, weil...
```

### Telling Stories

Purpose:

Telling stories allows learners to describe past events, experiences, problems, and sequences.

Why it matters:

Storytelling appears in daily life, work updates, doctor visits, travel conversations, school communication, and exams.

Common learner difficulties:

- Forgetting Perfekt structure.
- Mixing sequence connectors.
- Telling events without time markers.
- Losing the listener because the order is unclear.

Related situations:

- Talking about your weekend
- Describing a trip
- Reporting an accident
- Workplace update
- Doctor visit

Related communication functions:

- talking about the past
- explaining problems
- giving updates
- describing experiences
- reporting incidents

Useful patterns:

```text
Zuerst habe ich...
Dann bin ich...
Danach habe ich...
Am Ende war...
```

### Making Suggestions

Purpose:

Making suggestions helps learners propose actions, plans, solutions, or next steps.

Why it matters:

Suggestions are essential for social planning, family life, work, problem solving, and group discussions.

Common learner difficulties:

- Sounding too direct.
- Confusing suggestions with commands.
- Not using modal verbs or Konjunktiv II for softer phrasing.
- Not connecting the suggestion to a reason.

Related situations:

- Planning activities
- Team meetings
- Family decisions
- TELC speaking

Related communication functions:

- giving advice

### Agreeing And Disagreeing

Purpose:

Agreeing and disagreeing let learners respond to other people's ideas and participate in real conversation.

Why it matters:

Learners need more than answers. They need ways to join, soften, contrast, and continue discussion.

Common learner difficulties:

- Disagreeing too directly.
- Saying only yes or no without explanation.
- Not using contrast connectors.

## Product Principle

The Foundation Reference teaches how German works.

The Situation Library teaches where German is used.

Lessons teach how to use German in speaking, writing, retrieval, and real-world challenges.

The goal is not to complete a sequence.

The goal is to recognize patterns, understand the system, and use German with confidence.
