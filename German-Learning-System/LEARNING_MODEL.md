# Learning Model

## Product Design Specification

German Learning System is built around real-life communication. A lesson is not a grammar chapter with exercises attached. It is a guided journey from a recognizable situation to a usable communication outcome.

Each lesson should help the learner answer four product questions:

- Where would I use this?
- What can I say or understand now?
- How does the language fit together?
- Do I feel more confident using German in real life?

## Lesson Journey Overview

Every lesson follows the same core flow:

1. Real-life situation
2. Communication goal
3. Core vocabulary
4. Useful phrases
5. Guided speaking
6. Guided writing
7. Grammar in context
8. Retrieval practice
9. Real-world challenge
10. Reflection and confidence building

This consistent structure gives learners grounding. They know what kind of experience to expect, while the topic, language, and challenge evolve across levels.

## 1. Real-Life Situation

### Purpose

Anchor the lesson in a concrete moment from daily life, work, travel, health, housing, shopping, family, education, or social interaction.

The situation gives the learner a reason to care. It turns German from abstract study into a tool for action.

### Learner Experience

The learner recognizes the scenario and understands why the language matters. They should think, "I might actually need this."

The situation should feel specific enough to be useful, but broad enough to transfer to similar contexts.

### Example Activities

- Read a short scenario: "You are at the Apotheke and need something for a sore throat."
- Look at a photo, receipt, message, sign, or short dialogue from the situation.
- Choose what the learner needs to accomplish in the scenario.
- Identify what feels easy, difficult, or unfamiliar before learning begins.

### Expected Outcomes

- The learner understands the practical context.
- The learner can connect the lesson to a real need.
- The system creates motivation before introducing language.

## 2. Communication Goal

### Purpose

Define the observable outcome of the lesson. The communication goal tells the learner what they will be able to do by the end.

This keeps the lesson focused and prevents vocabulary, grammar, and activities from feeling disconnected.

### Learner Experience

The learner sees a clear target. They are not simply "learning dative" or "studying health words." They are learning to explain a symptom, ask for help, compare options, or make a request.

### Example Activities

- Present one can-do statement: "I can ask for medicine and explain one symptom."
- Show a short success example at the learner's level.
- Ask the learner to choose a personal version of the goal.
- Preview the final speaking or writing task.

### Expected Outcomes

- The learner knows what success looks like.
- The lesson has a clear product outcome.
- All later activities can be evaluated against the same goal.

## 3. Core Vocabulary

### Purpose

Introduce the minimum useful vocabulary needed for the situation. Vocabulary should be selected for immediate communicative value, not completeness.

The goal is to give learners enough language to act, not overwhelm them with every related word.

### Learner Experience

The learner meets words in meaningful groups and examples. They see how the words behave inside sentences and short exchanges.

### Example Activities

- Match words to images, actions, or short definitions.
- Sort vocabulary by function: symptoms, items, places, people, actions.
- Complete short sentence frames with new words.
- Listen and identify key words in a short dialogue.
- Mark words as "I know," "I recognize," or "I need to practice."

### Expected Outcomes

- The learner can recognize and use the most important words for the situation.
- Vocabulary is connected to actions, not memorized as an isolated list.
- The learner has enough language to begin guided production.

## 4. Useful Phrases

### Purpose

Give learners ready-to-use language chunks that help them communicate before they can build every sentence from scratch.

Useful phrases reduce friction and support early confidence.

### Learner Experience

The learner practices natural phrases they can reuse immediately. They begin to feel that German can be functional even when their grammar is still developing.

### Example Activities

- Repeat and personalize phrase frames.
- Choose the right phrase for a short situation.
- Build mini-dialogues from phrase blocks.
- Practice polite requests, clarification phrases, and repair strategies.
- Swap one detail in a phrase to create new meaning.

### Expected Outcomes

- The learner can use several practical phrases in the target situation.
- The learner has language for starting, continuing, and repairing communication.
- Speaking feels possible earlier in the lesson.

## 5. Guided Speaking

### Purpose

Move the learner from recognition into spoken use with support. Guided speaking turns input into action.

This step builds confidence through controlled success before asking for freer communication.

### Learner Experience

The learner speaks in small, manageable steps. They are not asked to improvise too early. Support gradually decreases as confidence increases.

### Example Activities

- Repeat short model lines with natural rhythm and pronunciation.
- Complete spoken sentence frames.
- Answer simple prompted questions.
- Practice a role play with visible support.
- Choose from phrase options to respond in a dialogue.
- Record and compare two attempts to notice improvement.

### Expected Outcomes

- The learner can say key phrases aloud with growing fluency.
- The learner can participate in a short supported exchange.
- The learner experiences speaking as practice, not performance.

## 6. Guided Writing

### Purpose

Use writing to slow down, organize language, and strengthen communication. Writing gives learners a second path to produce German clearly.

The writing task should support the same real-life situation as the speaking task.

### Learner Experience

The learner builds a useful text with structure and examples. They see how spoken language can become a message, note, form response, email, or short explanation.

### Example Activities

- Complete a short text message using a model.
- Fill in a form or appointment note.
- Write three sentences about the situation.
- Turn a spoken role play into a written message.
- Use a checklist to revise for clarity, key information, and tone.

### Expected Outcomes

- The learner can produce a short written response for the situation.
- The learner reinforces vocabulary and phrases through another mode.
- The learner gains a concrete artifact of progress.

## 7. Grammar in Context

### Purpose

Reveal the grammar pattern that helps the learner communicate more accurately or flexibly in the situation.

Grammar appears after the learner has seen and used meaningful language. It explains how the pieces fit together.

### Learner Experience

The learner notices a pattern inside familiar examples. The explanation should feel like a helpful key, not a detour into theory.

### Example Activities

- Highlight the pattern in the dialogue or phrases.
- Compare two examples and identify what changes.
- Complete a small pattern table based on lesson language.
- Fix common errors connected to the situation.
- Rebuild sentences by changing person, time, place, or object.

### Expected Outcomes

- The learner understands one focused grammar pattern in context.
- The learner can use the pattern to create new sentences.
- Grammar supports communication instead of interrupting it.

## 8. Retrieval Practice

### Purpose

Strengthen memory by bringing back language from earlier in the lesson and from previous lessons.

Retrieval practice helps learners connect scattered knowledge and build long-term fluency.

### Learner Experience

The learner recalls language actively instead of only rereading it. The task should feel short, achievable, and clearly connected to the communication goal.

### Example Activities

- Recall five useful words without looking.
- Rebuild a dialogue from prompts.
- Answer mixed review questions from current and past topics.
- Choose the right phrase for a familiar situation.
- Transform old language for the new scenario.
- Complete a quick "Can I still say this?" check.

### Expected Outcomes

- The learner can retrieve key language with less support.
- Older vocabulary and grammar remain active.
- The learner sees that new lessons build on what they already know.

## 9. Real-World Challenge

### Purpose

Transfer lesson language into an authentic or near-authentic task. This step connects classroom success to everyday confidence.

The challenge should be realistic, level-appropriate, and optional to complete in the real world when possible.

### Learner Experience

The learner applies German to something that feels useful beyond the lesson. They may complete the task independently, with a partner, or as a simulation.

### Example Activities

- Prepare and say one sentence at a cafe, shop, or Apotheke.
- Write a real or simulated message to make an appointment.
- Ask someone a simple question in German.
- Read a real sign, menu, receipt, website, or notice and identify key information.
- Role-play an unscripted version of the situation with new details.
- Create a personal phrase card for the next real encounter.

### Expected Outcomes

- The learner can transfer lesson language beyond the exercise format.
- The learner becomes less dependent on last-minute lookup.
- The lesson creates a bridge between practice and lived experience.

## 10. Reflection and Confidence Building

### Purpose

Help the learner notice progress, name what is becoming easier, and identify the next useful step.

Reflection turns a lesson into visible growth. Confidence is treated as a product outcome, not a side effect.

### Learner Experience

The learner ends with a sense of completion and direction. They should leave knowing what they can do, what still needs practice, and where this language could help them next.

### Example Activities

- Complete a confidence rating before and after the lesson.
- Answer: "What can I do now that I could not do before?"
- Choose one phrase to use in real life this week.
- Identify one word, phrase, or pattern to review later.
- Write a short learner reflection.
- Mark the communication goal as "not yet," "with support," or "independently."

### Expected Outcomes

- The learner can name their progress.
- The learner has a clear next action.
- The system reinforces motivation, agency, and confidence.

## Lesson Quality Checklist

A lesson is ready when it meets these criteria:

- The real-life situation is clear in the first minute.
- The communication goal is observable.
- Vocabulary is useful, limited, and contextual.
- Phrases are natural and reusable.
- Speaking begins before the learner has mastered every rule.
- Writing supports the same communication goal.
- Grammar explains a pattern the learner has already met.
- Retrieval practice includes both new and previous language.
- The real-world challenge feels possible.
- Reflection helps the learner see progress.

## Product Outcome

The lesson should leave the learner with more than new information. It should leave them with a small but real increase in ability.

The desired end state is:

- I understand where this language belongs.
- I can say or write something useful.
- I can connect this lesson to what I already know.
- I am more ready to use German outside the lesson.
